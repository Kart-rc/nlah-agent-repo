# Executive Communication

## Overview

Executives do not read documents; they extract decisions from them under time
pressure. A technically perfect analysis that buries its ask is
operationally worthless. This skill covers answer-first structure, the
one-page decision brief, and calibrated language — same substance, tuned to
the altitude of the reader.

## When to Use

- Finalizing a decision record, proposal, assessment, or brief for leadership
- Preparing to present a recommendation in a decision meeting
- Rewriting a practitioner-grade document for a VP or cross-org audience

**When NOT to use:** Working documents between practitioners, where
compression loses load-bearing detail. Do not summarize away the evidence in
the artifact of record — the brief points to it.

## Answer First (BLUF / Pyramid Principle)

State the conclusion in the first sentence, then support it in descending
order of importance, with detail available on demand.

- The reader should be able to stop after any paragraph and still leave with
  a correct (if shallower) understanding.
- Structure: **the answer → the three reasons → the evidence per reason**.
  Never the journey of how you got there; nobody is grading the homework.
- If the first sentence does not contain the ask or the verdict, the
  document is upside down.

## The One-Page Decision Brief

The highest-leverage executive artifact. Five sections, one page, no more:

1. **The ask** — the specific decision or resource requested, decidable
   yes/no.
2. **Recommendation** — one sentence, plus the single strongest reason.
3. **Cost** — money, people, time; ranges are fine, omissions are not.
4. **Primary risk** — the most likely way this goes wrong, and the
   mitigation. One honest risk builds more trust than five hedged ones.
5. **Decision needed by** — the date, and what happens by default if no
   decision is made.

## Pre-empt the Questions Executives Always Ask

Answer these inside the document, before they are asked:

- "What happens if we do nothing?"
- "What are the alternatives, and why do they lose?"
- "What does it cost — actually, all-in?"
- "What's the risk, and can we reverse it?"
- "Who else has signed off / disagrees?"

A brief that forces the reader to ask any of these has a hole where its
credibility should be. In Q&A, answer the question asked, first, in one
sentence — then add context if invited.

## Calibrated Uncertainty

Neither hedge nor overclaim; state confidence and its source.

- Bad: "This should probably improve latency somewhat."
- Bad: "This will cut latency in half." (unfounded precision)
- Good: "We expect 30-50% latency reduction, based on the pilot's measured
  38%; the range reflects production traffic mix."
- Every number gets a source or a label ("estimate", "measured", "vendor
  claim"). Executives forgive uncertainty; they do not forgive discovering it
  after the decision.

## Altitude Tailoring

Same substance, different depth:

- **Executive**: decisions, costs, risks, dates. Systems appear only where
  they change one of those.
- **Peer directors**: add ownership boundaries, cross-team dependencies,
  sequencing.
- **Practitioners**: add mechanics — but keep the answer first even here.
- The tell of wrong altitude: an executive brief naming libraries, or a
  practitioner doc with no implementation shape.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|---|---|
| "The context is necessary to understand the ask" | Then put the ask first and the context after. Readers who need it will keep reading. |
| "Simplifying is dumbing down" | Compression is selection, not distortion. The skill is choosing what matters at this altitude. |
| "They'll ask about the details anyway" | Good — answer from the appendix. That is what it is for. |
| "Hedging protects me if it goes wrong" | Hedging transfers the decision cost to the reader. Calibrated ranges protect you better. |

## Red Flags

- The ask appears on page two or later
- No "do nothing" consequence stated
- Costs without ranges, or ranges without sources
- Ten risks listed, none owned, none primary
- A brief longer than one page
- Uncertainty discovered by the audience rather than declared by the author

## Verification

- [ ] First sentence contains the answer or the ask
- [ ] Brief fits one page with all five sections
- [ ] The five standing executive questions are answered in-document
- [ ] Every number sourced or labeled; confidence stated with its basis
- [ ] Depth matches the declared audience
- [ ] Decision date and default stated
