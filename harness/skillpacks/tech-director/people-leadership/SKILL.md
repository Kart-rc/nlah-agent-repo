# People Leadership

## Overview

A director's output is the performance of their teams, not their own
artifacts. The technical work of the role — decisions, reviews, proposals —
succeeds or fails through people who must own, build, and operate the
results. This skill collects the practices that grow decision-makers instead
of dependents: calibrated delegation, feedback that lands, coaching over
prescribing, and reading team health before it becomes attrition.

## When to Use

- Drafting a growth plan, feedback notes, or a difficult-conversation opener
- Deciding what (and how much) to delegate, and to whom
- Reviewing an initiative whose risks are organizational: ownership, morale,
  bus factor, team boundaries
- Diagnosing why a capable team is deciding slowly or shipping timidly

**When NOT to use:** Compensation, performance-management process, or legal
matters — those follow your organization's HR frameworks, not a practice
skill. This skill informs judgment; it does not replace policy.

## Delegation Levels

Delegation is not binary; mismatched levels cause both micromanagement and
abandonment. Match the level to the person's readiness *for this specific
kind of work*:

1. **Task** — "do exactly this"; for the new or the critical-and-urgent.
2. **Outcome** — "achieve this result; the approach is yours"; the default
   for competent engineers.
3. **Area** — "you own this domain; tell me what I should know"; for
   emerging leaders.

- **Delegate the problem, not the solution.** Handing someone your solution
  to implement is task-level delegation wearing outcome-level clothes.
- State the level explicitly, including decision rights: what they decide
  alone, what they decide-and-inform, what needs consultation.
- If you cannot delegate a decision because only you have the context, the
  finding is about you: distribute the context.

## Feedback That Lands

- **SBI**: Situation ("in Tuesday's design review"), Behavior ("you
  dismissed the junior engineer's question without answering it"), Impact
  ("they went quiet for the rest of the meeting, and we lost their input").
  Observable behavior, not inferred character — never "you're dismissive."
- **Praise publicly, correct privately** — and deliver both close to the
  event. Frequency beats ceremony: ten small course corrections outperform
  one annual review surprise.
- Feedback is a two-way channel: ask for it, act on it visibly, and the
  team's feedback to each other improves to match.

## Coaching Over Prescribing

- Questions before answers: "what have you considered?", "what would you do
  if I were on vacation?", "what's the risk you're most worried about?"
  The goal is their judgment getting stronger, not your answer getting
  implemented.
- **Push decisions down** with support: let engineers make the calls they
  can safely make, reviewing the *decision quality* afterwards rather than
  intercepting the decision beforehand.
- Growing decision-makers requires letting people make recoverable mistakes.
  Rescuing every two-way-door error teaches dependence; reserve intervention
  for one-way doors.
- Blameless retrospection is a leadership behavior, not a meeting format:
  when things fail, model hunting for causes in the system before causes in
  the person — the team's honesty in the next incident depends on it.

## Team Health Signals

Watch leading indicators, not just delivery metrics:

- **Decision latency** — how long do decisions sit unmade? Rising latency
  signals unclear ownership or fear of being wrong.
- **Dissent frequency** — healthy teams argue in the open. Silence in design
  reviews is a worse sign than conflict.
- **Attrition risk** — growth conversations gone stale, scope shrinking,
  the strongest engineer going quiet. Exit interviews are lagging
  indicators; skip-levels and 1:1s are leading ones.
- **Bus factor** — systems only one person can operate are organizational
  risks wearing technical clothes; they belong on risk registers.
- 1:1s are the collection mechanism: their agenda belongs to the report, and
  "status" is the least valuable thing to spend them on.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|---|---|
| "It's faster to do it myself" | This week, yes. Every week, it caps your team at the size of your calendar. |
| "They're not ready to own it" | Readiness is built by owning things slightly too big, with support. Waiting for readiness produces none. |
| "I don't want to demotivate them with criticism" | Withheld feedback is a decision to let them fail later, in public, at higher stakes. |
| "The team is fine — delivery is on track" | Delivery is a lagging indicator. Teams deliver on track for two quarters after they have already checked out. |

## Red Flags

- Every decision on the director's desk; decision latency measured in weeks
- 1:1s that are status meetings, cancelled first when calendars compress
- Feedback that describes character ("sloppy", "brilliant") rather than behavior
- A "delegated" outcome with hourly check-ins — or a delegated area never
  discussed again
- Post-incident reviews that quietly hunt for the person who typed the command
- Growth plans that are promotion checklists rather than capability plans

## Verification

- [ ] Delegations state level, decision rights, and check-in cadence
- [ ] Feedback in SBI form, tied to observable behavior, delivered promptly
- [ ] Decisions pushed to the lowest level that can safely make them
- [ ] Team-health signals (decision latency, dissent, bus factor, attrition risk) reviewed on a cadence
- [ ] People risks appear on initiative risk registers, not just in your head
