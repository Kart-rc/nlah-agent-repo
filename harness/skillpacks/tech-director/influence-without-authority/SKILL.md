# Influence Without Authority

## Overview

Past a certain scope, no one can order the organization to move; directors
ship through teams they do not manage. This skill covers reading the
stakeholder landscape accurately, building the minimum coalition an
initiative needs, and sequencing conversations so decisions are made before
the meeting that ratifies them.

The core ethic: influence is trading in genuine interests, done in the open.
Every move in an influence plan should survive being read by its target.

## When to Use

- An initiative needs teams outside your authority to adopt, fund, or build
- A proposal will land on desks with competing priorities
- A decision meeting is approaching and positions are unknown or opposed
- A previous attempt at the same initiative died for non-technical reasons

**When NOT to use:** Decisions inside your own scope (just decide), or
situations where the real gap is substance, not alignment — a weak proposal
pre-wired is still a weak proposal.

## Stakeholder Mapping

Map everyone who can **block, fund, or must execute** the initiative:

- For each: their **stake** (what this costs or gains them, in their terms),
  their **position** on a spectrum — champion / supporter / neutral /
  skeptic / opponent — and the **evidence** for that read (something they
  said or did, not something you hope).
- A position without evidence is a guess; label it as assumed and plan to
  verify it early.
- Power/interest sorting: high-power high-interest stakeholders get
  conversations; high-power low-interest get concise briefs; low-power
  high-interest get honest engagement (today's skeptical senior engineer is
  next year's blocking architect).

## The Minimum Winning Coalition

You do not need everyone; you need enough.

- Name which positions must move (e.g. "the platform VP from skeptic to
  neutral; the data lead from neutral to supporter") for the initiative to
  proceed.
- Name which oppositions you can **accept as unresolved** — and check the
  initiative genuinely survives them.
- Aim influence effort at the coalition, not at converting the loudest
  opponent. Some opposition is cheaper to accept than to convert.

## Pre-wiring

Never let a decision-maker meet your proposal for the first time in the
meeting that decides it.

- Sequence 1:1 conversations before any forum: hear objections while they
  are cheap, adapt, and let stakeholders shape the proposal enough to own a
  piece of it.
- The decision meeting then *ratifies* what the coalition already supports —
  surprise in that room is a planning failure.
- Sequencing matters: early conversations with likely supporters build
  momentum and intelligence for the harder ones.

## Objections and Concessions

- **Steelman the strongest opponent** — state their objection so they would
  endorse the phrasing. Then answer with substance: a concession, a pilot, a
  changed scope, data. Messaging is not an answer.
- **Plan real concessions.** An influence plan that trades nothing is a
  broadcast plan. What will you give up — scope, credit, timeline, control —
  to get what matters?
- If the honest answer to an objection is "you're right, in your case this
  is a net loss," say that, and compensate elsewhere or shrink the ask.

## Escalation as a Designed Move

Escalation is a legitimate tool with criteria, not a relationship failure:

- Escalate when positions are irreconcilable at your level *and* the
  decision is time-bound — with the opponent's position represented fairly
  (ideally jointly written).
- Define in advance: the conditions under which alignment is abandoned, who
  the escalation goes to, and what you will ask them to decide.
- An escalation that surprises the other party costs more than it wins.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|---|---|
| "The idea is right; it'll win on merit" | Merit is necessary, not sufficient. Organizations adopt what they co-own. |
| "Mapping stakeholders is politics" | Ignoring stakeholders is also politics — just the losing kind. Mapping in the open is respect. |
| "We'll handle objections in the meeting" | The meeting is the worst room to hear an objection for the first time — for you and for them. |
| "Escalating means I failed" | Escalating on criteria, jointly framed, is how disagreements between peers are supposed to resolve. |

## Red Flags

- A stakeholder map missing whoever actually signs off
- Position reads based on hope, with no marked assumptions
- A plan with no concessions and no answer for the strongest opponent
- All influence moves undated and unowned
- A decision meeting scheduled before any 1:1 has happened
- Tactics you would be uncomfortable showing to their targets

## Verification

- [ ] Everyone who can block, fund, or must execute is mapped, with evidence or a marked assumption
- [ ] Minimum winning coalition named; accepted oppositions listed
- [ ] Strongest objection steelmanned and answered with substance
- [ ] Every move dated, owned, with an explicit ask and a fallback
- [ ] Concessions identified (or their absence flagged as a risk)
- [ ] Escalation criteria and path defined
